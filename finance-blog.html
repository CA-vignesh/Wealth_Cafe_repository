The Index Fund Illusion
📅 March 31, 2026 ✍️ WealthCafe Research 📖 5 min read

Own 3 Index Funds? Look Closer!

More funds don't always mean more diversification; broad indices overlap far more than you think.

Breadth on paper, heft at the top:

Broad-market indices are weighted by market cap — bigger companies get bigger weight and drive returns. So even when indices differ in stock count, they share the same anchors.

Adding more index funds leads to very small incremental improvement, not meaningful diversification.

Equal-weighted indices reduce dependence on a handful of large-caps and offer broader participation, genuinely different exposure, not just a bigger basket of the same stocks — thereby offering meaningful diversification.

🎯 Key Takeaway

Diversification isn't measured by the number of funds you own — it's measured by the uniqueness of exposure they provide.

Why Long-Term SIP Success Depends More on Time Than Timing
📅 June 2026 ✍️ WealthCafe Research 📖 6 min read

Many investors delay starting SIPs, assuming markets are "too expensive." But does a high Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio really impact long-term wealth creation?

An analysis of 277 months (April 2003 – April 2026) using the BSE Sensex P/E ratio classified markets as:

✅
Undervalued: P/E below 5-year average by more than 10%
❌
Overvalued: P/E above 5-year average by more than 10%
Key observations:
  • SIPs started in undervalued markets had a 56% chance of delivering >20% annualized returns in the first year, with only a 3.5% chance of losses.
  • SIPs started in overvalued markets had a 28% chance of a first-year loss, and the likelihood of >20% annualized returns was lower at 35%.
  • This early advantage reduced steadily over time.
  • After 10–15 years, SIPs started in overvalued markets delivered almost the same average annual returns as those started in undervalued markets.

🏆 Key Takeaways
📈
In both cases, the majority of SIPs ultimately generated 10–20% annualized returns, highlighting the power of discipline and compounding.
🕐
A high P/E ratio is useful for setting short-term expectations — not for deciding whether to begin investing. Valuations influence the journey, but time in the market determines the destination.
⏳
For long-term investors, the biggest risk isn't starting at a high valuation — it's waiting on the sidelines for the "perfect" entry point and missing years of compounding.
